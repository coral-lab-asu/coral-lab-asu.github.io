DeALOG

Decentralized · Multi-Agent · Log-Mediated Reasoning
A Framework for Complex Question Answering

A planner-free multi-agent framework that reasons over tables, text, and images through a shared natural-language log.

Complex question answering across heterogeneous sources requires both specialization and coordination. DeALOG replaces the brittle central planner of conventional agent pipelines with a lightweight scheduler and a shared, auditable log — letting specialist agents contribute, verify, and correct one another without a single point of failure. The result: competitive accuracy on six benchmarks, graceful degradation under 30% evidence corruption, and transparent reasoning you can actually inspect.

§ 01   The Argument

Between chain-of-thought and the brittle planner.

Modern multi-hop question answering sits awkwardly between two regimes. Single-LLM chain-of-thought reasons inside one context window — effective for shallow queries but unstable as chains grow long and partial results accumulate. Planner-based pipelines externalize reasoning into explicit task graphs, gaining stepwise structure but concentrating uncertainty in a central planner whose early errors propagate downstream.

DeALOG sidesteps both by doing something architecturally different: it removes the planner entirely. A lightweight scheduler manages when agents run, not what they do. Specialist agents read from and append to a shared, append-only natural-language log — contributing evidence, summarizing, verifying, flagging. Coordination is emergent; verification is peer-to-peer; provenance is auditable by construction.

Under 30% evidence corruption, a re-planning planner's EM drops by 24.7 points. DeALOG degrades by only 6.4 points. On operator chains of length 8+, DeALOG sustains 0.77 EM where the planner collapses to 0.50. This is what distributed, log-mediated reasoning buys you.

§ 02   The Method

Specialists, a scheduler, and a shared log.

Five specialist agents operate zero-/few-shot over tabular, textual, and visual inputs. Each implements a should_act(log) trigger and an act(log) LLM call that appends typed entries — LOOKUP, QUOTE, VISUAL, SUMMARY, ANSWER, FLAG/OK — to an append-only log with provenance metadata. The scheduler offers turns, enforces guardrails, deduplicates via ROUGE-L similarity, and decides when to stop.

A-01
SpecialistTableAgent
Parses structured tables and posts cell-level LOOKUP facts with row/column provenance.
A-02
SpecialistContextAgent
Retrieves and quotes relevant textual passages via BM25+miniLM; abstains when no evidence surfaces.
A-03
SpecialistVisualAgent
Converts charts, diagrams, and OCR output into text via BLIP-2 + PaddleOCR for downstream consumption.
C-01
CoordinatorSummarizingAgent
Synthesizes the final answer from log state — or requests missing evidence if the picture is incomplete.
C-02
CoordinatorVerificationAgent
Cross-checks arithmetic, units, and factual grounding against log entries; flags inconsistencies for one re-engagement.
Figure 1  ·  Specialist agents (Table, Context, Visual) append typed evidence entries to the shared log. Coordinator agents (Summarizing, Verification) read the log, synthesize answers, and trigger one-shot re-engagement when inconsistencies are flagged. No plan, no central controller — coordination is emergent.

Scheduler vs. Planner

The DeALOG controller operates at the resource-management level with zero semantic awareness — analogous to an OS process scheduler. Central planners, by contrast, operate at the reasoning-control level. Formalized across nine dimensions:

§ 04   The Case

When the plan breaks, the log holds.

Under corruption, decentralization wins.

A re-planning Planner loses 24.7 EM points when 30% of evidence entries are corrupted. A Plan→Log hybrid softens the blow but still propagates early errors through its plan. DeALOG — with no plan to corrupt — degrades by only:

6.4pts

On long-horizon operator chains (8+ ops), the pattern intensifies. Planner collapses to 0.50 EM; DeALOG holds 0.77. Peer verification catches arithmetic and unit errors at 88% detection rate; the one-shot re-engagement corrects many omissions without causing reasoning loops.
